Humidity in the Operating Room

Best Practices for Surgical Technologists — Relative Humidity, ESD, Sterile Supply Integrity & Risk Assessment

ASHRAE 170 NFPA 99 CMS Compliance IFU Requirements AST Standards

Overview & Rationale

Why relative humidity in the OR matters — and how the standards have evolved.

🎯
Core Purpose: Maintaining the correct level of relative humidity (RH) in the OR is essential to control microbial growth, prevent electrostatic discharge (ESD), preserve sterile supply integrity, protect electro-medical devices (EMDs), and ensure OR team comfort.
Current ASHRAE-Approved RH Range for Anesthetizing Locations
20% – 60%
Revised 2008 · Adopted by NFPA 99 (2012), CMS (2013), and Facility Guidelines Institute
30%
Former minimum RH standard (pre-2008)
20%
Current CMS minimum RH for anesthetizing locations (since 2013)
$200M
Estimated industry savings over 10 years from the lower RH standard

Historical Timeline

Pre-2008
30% Minimum RH
The longstanding standard. Set to aid microbial control, prevent ESD, and maintain OR team comfort. Also required by NFPA 99 (1999 edition: >35% RH in anesthetizing locations).
2008 · ASHRAE Revision
Lower Limit Dropped to 20%
ASHRAE Addendum D to Standard 170 expanded the minimum RH from 30% to 20% — driven by HDO challenges in cold/dry climates and costly HVAC requirements.
2012 · NFPA 99
ASHRAE Standard Adopted
NFPA 99 eliminated direct RH references and cross-referenced ASHRAE Standard 170. Facility Guidelines Institute followed suit in the 2014 edition.
April 2013 · CMS
Federal Compliance Updated
CMS officially lowered the minimum RH requirement for anesthetizing locations from >35% to 20%, allowing HDOs nationwide to set the lower end at 20%.
Summer 2014 · Key Event
CMS Surveyor Citation
A hospital was cited because RH fell below the level stated in a sterile product's IFU — revealing a critical oversight: the standard change did not account for product-specific IFU requirements.
January 2015 · Joint Comm.
Multi-Org Joint Communication
13+ organizations (AST, AORN, AAMI, ASHRAE, AHA, ASA, etc.) issued a joint communication to HDOs about IFU compliance and the risks of lower RH on sterile supplies and EMDs.
⚠️
Critical Oversight: The ASHRAE standard change to 20% did NOT address the impact on supplies and equipment used in anesthetizing locations. Manufacturer IFUs — which may still require 30–60% RH — must be consulted and followed independently of ASHRAE standards.

Why the Old 30% Standard Was Relaxed

  • Development and widespread use of non-flammable anesthetic gases reduced fire risk concerns
  • Improved ventilation system technology changed OR airflow management
  • Improved materials in sterile drapes and gowns reduced flammability and ESD contribution
  • Many HDOs — particularly in cold-weather and arid regions — found maintaining ≥30% RH extremely difficult and expensive
  • Additional HVAC equipment needed to maintain higher RH incurred installation and ongoing maintenance costs

AST Guidelines I – III

The three formal guidelines and their associated standards for CSTs and healthcare delivery organizations.

Guideline I
Establish RH Levels in Compliance with Federal Regulations & IFUs
▼
📌
Core Requirement: HDOs should establish OR RH levels that comply with both federal regulations AND the IFUs for all supplies and equipment stored or used in the OR — these may differ.
  • A coordinated team effort is required — CSTs, HTMs (formerly "biomedical technicians"), RNs, and environmental services must all participate
  • The team should complete a risk assessment to identify relevant scientific and technical issues before establishing RH policies
  • Risk assessment results and committee decisions must be documented
  • CSTs and all OR personnel must know and understand the IFUs for all supplies and equipment stored and used in the OR
  • The HDO's safety or environment committee should be involved — not just facilities management
Key Point: Federal standards set the minimum RH range. Manufacturer IFUs set the specific RH requirements for each product. Both must be followed — and IFUs may be more restrictive than federal standards.
Guideline II
Annual Review of Humidity Policies & Procedures
▼
  • The surgery department must review all policies and procedures (P&Ps) for OR humidity on an annual basis
  • Reviews must include a multidisciplinary group: CSTs, surgeons, RNs, risk management, and the infection control officer
  • The department must document: when P&Ps were reviewed, what revisions were made (if any), and who participated
  • All CSTs must be familiar with current P&Ps for humidity in the OR
  • New employee orientation must include reviewing the humidity P&Ps
📄
Documentation Requirement: All reviews must be formally documented — including dates, participants, and any revisions. This is a regulatory and accreditation requirement.
Guideline III
Continuing Education for CSTs on OR Humidity
▼
  • CSTs must complete continuing education (CE) to remain current in their knowledge of OR humidity standards
  • CE should be based on andragogy — the adult learning theory by Malcolm Knowles — where education is relevant, practical, and learner-active
  • Surgery departments should use varied instructional methods to facilitate learning
  • If primarily lecture-based, supplement with case study discussions and audience participation
  • Proven alternatives include: interactive training videos, computerized training modules, and teleconferences
  • CE should be delivered in short modules, not one lengthy session
  • CE programs should be periodically evaluated for effectiveness, including participant feedback
Education Records Must Be Maintained for a Minimum of 3 Years and include: dates of sessions, names and job titles of participants, a synopsis of each session, and names/credentials/experience of instructors.
Adult Learning (Andragogy)
Malcolm Knowles Principles
Adults learn best when: information is relevant to their work experience; material is practical rather than purely academic; the learner is actively involved in the process.
CE Records — 3-Year Minimum
Required Documentation
Dates of education · Names & job titles of attendees · Synopsis of each session · Instructor names, credentials, and experience.

Clinical Impacts of Low Humidity

How RH levels below 30% affect sterile supplies, electro-medical devices, patient safety, and OR personnel.

🚨
Core Risk: Reducing OR RH below the level specified in a product's IFU can compromise sterile supply integrity, cause EMD malfunction, and create ESD hazards — even if the facility is in compliance with federal standards.

Impact on Sterile Supplies

Shelf Life
Shortened or Compromised
Manufacturers test products at a specific RH (assumed to be 30%). If stored at a lower RH than the IFU specifies, the shelf life of sterile supplies can be shortened or invalidated.
Product Integrity
Package & Performance Affected
Lower RH can compromise the integrity of sterile packaging and the performance of humidity-sensitive products — regardless of whether federal RH minimums are met.
Humidity-Sensitive Products
High-Risk Items to Know
  • Biological indicators — used for sterilization monitoring
  • Chemical indicators — used for sterilization monitoring
  • EKG electrodes — packaged in foil pouches specifically to protect against humidity changes
  • Electrosurgical consumables — some are humidity-sensitive
Manufacturer Testing Gap
Transition Challenge
Manufacturers must re-test all products at the lower RH — a time-consuming and equipment-intensive process. Until products are re-tested and re-cleared, the original IFU requirement stands.

Impact on Electro-Medical Devices (EMDs)

Malfunction Risk
Older EMDs Most Vulnerable
EMDs — particularly older models — may malfunction unexpectedly at RH levels below their IFU requirements. Low humidity can also affect calibration accuracy. Capital equipment with long intended lifespans may not be compatible with newer lower RH standards.
ESD to Equipment
Internal Component Damage
Larger electrostatic discharge (ESD) pulses at low RH can destroy internal parts of EMDs, cause premature failure, and produce erratic software behavior due to "confusion" from ESD pulses. Lower RH = larger potential ESD events.
Mixed Device Fleet
No Universal RH Applies
Most ORs use a mix of old and new EMDs from multiple manufacturers. A standard RH policy cannot be assumed to cover all devices. Each device's IFU must be reviewed independently. New equipment trends toward accepting lower RH levels.

Impact on OR Personnel & Fire Safety

Personnel Safety
Electrostatic Shock Risk
In low-humidity environments, OR staff can more easily become "charged" and receive electrostatic shocks when contacting EMDs. Dr. Farhad Memarzadeh (NIH) notes no documented cases in MAUDE or ECRI databases of static-caused patient harm or device fire at 20% RH — though vigilance is warranted.
Surgical Fire Triangle
ESD as Ignition Source
ESD can serve as an ignition source completing the surgical fire triangle: ignition source + oxygen + fuel. Low RH increases ESD potential. Non-flammable anesthetics have reduced (but not eliminated) this concern.
IFU Rule: It is uncertain for how long RH can remain below a manufacturer's minimum before equipment or product integrity is affected. Do not assume short-term deviations are safe — contact the manufacturer for excursion data.

HVAC & Facility Challenges

  • RH is intimately tied to outdoor air and local climate — facilities in arid regions or areas with extreme seasonal variation face ongoing challenges maintaining higher RH levels
  • Humidification systems take time to compensate when weather conditions change abruptly — the nominal "setpoint" may not be maintained during transitions
  • Installing additional HVAC humidification equipment to maintain ≥30% RH is expensive and requires ongoing maintenance
  • ASHRAE 170 addresses design standards but does not require 100% operational compliance at design goals at all times
  • If the decision is made to allow RH below 30%, consider moving sensitive supplies to a humidity-controlled storage area

Risk Assessment Framework

8-step process for HDOs considering OR RH levels below 30% — from the AST/AAMI Joint Communication (January 2015).

📋
Before Establishing RH Below 30%: Healthcare facilities must conduct a thorough risk assessment addressing the equipment and supplies in use. The following 8 questions are the required framework per the joint communication.

The 8-Step Risk Assessment Questions

1
Desired vs. Achievable RH
What is the desired minimum humidity level and range in the OR — and what RH can the HVAC system actually achieve and maintain across a variety of weather conditions? These may differ significantly.
2
Historical RH Data Analysis
Have you assessed humidity level data over a sufficient time period (covering all seasonal variations) to know whether, when, and for how long humidity falls below 30% due to environmental conditions? Conducted in consultation with facilities engineers.
3
IFU Inventory Audit
Have you determined what the IFUs specify for humidity levels for each item in the HDO's existing inventory of supplies and equipment used in the OR? This includes every sterile supply and every EMD.
4
Risk of Using Out-of-Range Equipment
What are the likely risks of using equipment that calls for ≥30% RH (especially older EMDs)? What are the potential impacts on device performance, sterility, and patient safety?
5
Manufacturer Excursion Data
Request from manufacturers the variance of time ("excursion data") that products can be out of range before package integrity or performance is affected. Note: this data may not yet be available from all manufacturers.
6
Planned New Supplies & Equipment
For any planned new supplies and equipment purchases, what are the anticipated recommended humidity levels for both storage and use? Factor this into procurement decisions.
7
Overall Benefit-Risk Analysis
Using all available information, has an overall assessment been conducted to determine whether the benefits of lowering the humidity level threshold below 30% override the potential risks to patients and equipment?
8
Humidity-Controlled Storage
If the decision is made to maintain OR humidity below 30%, consider relocating supplies that require ≥30% RH to a dedicated humidity-controlled storage closet. Note: EKG electrodes and other humidity-sensitive items are used throughout the facility — the same assessment applies to other clinical areas.

Who Should Be Involved in the Risk Assessment

Role Contribution to Risk Assessment
CST (Certified Surgical Technologist) Monitors and documents RH levels; reports IFU compliance concerns; serves on the committee; identifies humidity-sensitive supplies in daily use
HTM (Healthcare Technology Management) Evaluates EMD IFUs; reports device performance issues at lower RH; advises on calibration and ESD risk; assesses mixed-age device fleet
RN / Perioperative Nurse Contributes clinical perspective; helps identify patient safety implications; participates in P&P development
Facilities / HVAC Engineers Evaluates what RH the HVAC system can realistically achieve; provides historical RH monitoring data; assesses seasonal variation
Infection Control Officer Reviews microbial growth implications; evaluates sterilization monitoring product IFU compliance
Risk Management Reviews liability and regulatory exposure; ensures documentation; coordinates accreditation compliance
Surgeons / Clinical Leadership Provide clinical input; involved in annual P&P review; final patient safety oversight
✅
Two Goals of All Stakeholders: (1) Ensure patients are protected through the safe and effective use of equipment and products during surgery. (2) Eliminate waste of resources for installation, energy, and maintenance that do not improve patient outcomes.

Key Terms & Glossary

Definitions as established by AST Guidelines for Humidity in the Operating Room (Approved 2015, Revised 2017).

Relative Humidity (RH)
The amount of water vapor present in air expressed as a percentage of the amount needed for saturation at the same temperature. In the OR, the accepted range is 20–60% per ASHRAE Standard 170.
Humidity
A quantity representing the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere or a gas.
Electro-Medical Device (EMD)
An electronic instrument or equipment used for medical purposes and health care. EMDs may be sensitive to lower RH levels, particularly older models; IFUs must be consulted for each device.
Shelf Life
The length of time for which an item remains usable, sterile, and fit for use. Lower-than-specified RH can shorten the shelf life of sterile supplies and compromise sterile barrier integrity.
Electrostatic Discharge (ESD)
A sudden flow of electricity between two electrically charged objects. In low-humidity environments, ESD events are more frequent and more powerful — posing risks to EMD components and potentially completing the surgical fire triangle.
Instructions for Use (IFU)
Manufacturer-provided documentation specifying the required conditions for safe storage and use of a product, including environmental humidity requirements. Also called Directions for Use (DFU). IFUs are legally and clinically binding.
Healthcare Delivery Organization (HDO)
Any healthcare facility or organization responsible for developing, approving, and implementing policies for its clinical environment — including OR humidity standards, based on federal regulations and IFUs.
Healthcare Technology Management (HTM)
Previously called "biomedical technicians." HTMs manage and maintain electro-medical devices and are key stakeholders in OR humidity risk assessment — particularly regarding EMD IFU requirements and ESD risk.
Andragogy
The theory of adult learning, developed by Malcolm Knowles. Key principles: adults learn best when material is relevant to their work, practical rather than academic, and when learners are actively involved in the learning process. Required basis for CST continuing education.
ASHRAE Standard 170
Ventilation of Health Care Facilities. The standard that established the 20–60% RH range for anesthetizing locations. Adopted by NFPA 99 (2012) and CMS (2013). Addresses design standards — not 100% operational compliance at all times.
Excursion Data
Manufacturer data documenting how long a product can remain outside its specified RH range before integrity or performance is compromised. This data may not be available for all products — contact the manufacturer directly.
Surgical Fire Triangle
The three elements required for a surgical fire: an ignition source (e.g., ESD or electrosurgery) + oxygen + fuel (e.g., drapes, gowns, prep agents). Low RH increases ESD risk, potentially completing this triangle.
HVAC
Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning system. Responsible for maintaining OR environmental conditions including temperature and RH. Achieving and maintaining specific RH levels can be costly and difficult in arid or cold climates.
